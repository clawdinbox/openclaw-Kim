## Contrarian Take

The consensus narrative is that premium will hold because luxury has held. This misses a critical distinction: **true luxury has held; diffused premium has not.**

The error is conflating price point with positioning. A $400 dress is not luxury just because of its price. If it's available at 40% off within six weeks, it's mid-market with a premium price tagâ€”and it's precisely the category about to experience the next phase of compression.

The other missed signal: outlet dependency. Brands that built growth on outlet revenue (often 30-40% of total sales) trained consumers to wait for discounts. They don't have pricing power; they have pricing theater. As consumer budgets tighten and quality at lower price points rises (Zara's fabric investments, Uniqlo's technical partnerships), the "pay more for slightly better" proposition collapses.

## So What for Marcel

**Immediate positioning implications for client conversations:**

1. **Audit outlet exposure.** If a brand derives >20% of revenue from outlets, they have a structural problem, not a channel mix problem. The discount expectation has been internalized by their customer base.

2. **Test vertical integration feasibility.** Can development cycles compress? Is nearshoring viable? The brands that survive this shift will control more of their value chain, not less.

3. **Clarify the ladder.** Either move up (scarcity, full-price discipline, true luxury codes) or move down (speed, value transparency, operational excellence). The middle is vanishing.
